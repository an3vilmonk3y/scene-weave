The Last Ferry

The rain started somewhere over the Irish Sea, and by the time Nora's train pulled into the coastal station at Aberford it had become the kind of steady, serious downpour that makes the world feel smaller. She pressed her forehead against the cold glass and watched the platform lights smear into pale gold streaks across the window.

She had not been back in seven years. The town looked the same — the low white-washed buildings, the fish-and-chip shop with its yellow awning, the weathered sign for the Anchor Hotel swinging above the road that curved down toward the harbour. It looked the same the way a childhood photograph looks the same: familiar but subtly wrong in scale, as though someone had shrunk everything slightly while she wasn't looking.

She hauled her bag from the overhead rack and stepped out onto the platform. The rain smelled of seaweed and cold stone.

---

The Anchor's bar was almost empty at six in the evening. A couple of fishermen occupied the corner table, speaking low over their pints. The barman, a young man Nora didn't recognise, was polishing glasses with the meditative focus of someone who had run out of other things to do. A fire burned in the old stone hearth, throwing amber light across the flagstone floor and the mismatched chairs arranged around it.

Nora took a stool at the bar and ordered a whisky. The room was warm and smelled of woodsmoke and old timber and something faintly briny underneath, the way everything in Aberford smelled of the sea eventually.

It was there, in the mirror behind the bottles, that she saw him.

Daniel was sitting alone at a table near the window, both hands wrapped around a mug of something hot, watching the harbour lights rock in the water below. He was thinner than she remembered, and his hair had grown out, dark and a little too long. He had the look of a man who had been somewhere difficult and was not entirely back yet.

She did not move for a long moment. In the mirror, he hadn't seen her.

---

Outside, the rain had eased to a mist. The harbour was quiet — just the soft knock of boats against the dock and the low pulse of a foghorn somewhere out past the breakwater. The cobblestones shone black and wet under the iron lamp-posts, and the lights of the few boats still moored threw long pale reflections across the water.

Daniel had come to stand beside her. Neither of them had really decided this was happening; it had simply happened, the way things tend to when two people are avoiding a conversation they are already having.

"You could have called," he said.

"I know."

The foghorn sounded again, lower and more distant now.

"I wasn't sure you'd want me to," she said.

He was quiet for a while. A gull crossed the mouth of the harbour in a slow arc, white against the dark grey sky, then disappeared beyond the breakwater wall.

"I don't know what I wanted," he said at last. "That was the problem."

---

The lighthouse had been decommissioned for years, but the old keeper's cottage at its base was still intact — barely. The door had swollen in its frame and required a shoulder to open, and once inside there was the particular cold of a room that had not been heated in a long time. But the roof held, and someone had left an oil lamp on the shelf, and there was a box of matches beside it.

Daniel lit the lamp while Nora found a pair of wooden chairs and dragged them close to the small iron stove in the corner. There was no fuel for the stove, so they sat with their coats on, which made it feel oddly like an adventure.

The lamp threw soft yellow light across the stone walls. Through the single window, the sea was a dark restless mass beneath a sky that had cleared a little — a few stars showing between the moving clouds.

"I used to come here when I was sixteen," Nora said. "When I needed to think."

"I know. You told me."

"Did I?" She looked at him. "I don't remember telling you that."

"You told me a lot of things." He turned the lamp's adjustment ring slightly, bringing the flame up. "You just don't remember which ones."

---

The ferry left at half seven in the morning. They stood at the end of the dock in the thin early light, watching it manoeuvre out of the harbour mouth, its wake spreading in white arcs across the still grey water. The sky was the colour of pewter streaked with the first pale suggestion of sun somewhere far to the east.

Neither of them was leaving on it. That had become clear somewhere around two in the morning, sitting on the cold floor of the lighthouse cottage with the lamp burning low between them, talking the way people only talk when they have run out of things to protect.

The ferry passed the breakwater and straightened its course. Nora watched it go.

"There's a decent breakfast place on Church Street," she said. "If it's still there."

"Moran's," he said. "It's still there."

She picked up her bag from where she'd set it on the dock. "Then we should probably go before the tables fill up."

He fell into step beside her. The cobblestones were damp and the morning smelled of salt and coffee and something baking somewhere nearby. The town was waking up around them, shutters opening, a van backing slowly down a narrow lane, a dog pulling its owner toward the waterfront at a purposeful trot.

It was not forgiveness, exactly. It was not a resolution. It was more like an opening — the way a door that has been stuck for years will sometimes move, suddenly, without warning, if you simply try it again at the right moment.

Nora didn't say any of this. She just walked, and he walked beside her, and the town carried on being itself around them in the early morning light.
